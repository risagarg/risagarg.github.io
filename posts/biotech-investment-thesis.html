How I Built My Biotech Investment Thesis (as a student)

April 30, 2025

Lessons from labs, startups, and the space in between

Last summer, I spent my mornings culturing cancer cells and my afternoons in meetings with the CEO. I was a research intern at a biotech startup focused on engineering oncolytic viruses. But what stayed with me the most wasn't just the TCID50 assays or qPCR curves, it was watching how science becomes a company in real time. It was watching how fast things break, how fast they get fixed, and how founders handle the space in between.

That was when I started building my investment thesis. Not intentionally, and I didn't even know to call it that yet. But I started noticing patterns: why some teams moved faster, why some ideas attracted funding, and why some experiments felt like they were pointing toward something bigger. I wasn't writing memos. I was trying to keep up. But the questions stuck.

1. The Team

One of the first lessons I learned – extremely fast – is that startups are not orderly workplaces. The founders I worked with weren't just "experienced." They were obsessed with their idea. They were the kind of people who could go from a failed assay to a pivoted protocol in an afternoon and still show up with energy at the next investor call. They weren't waiting for clarity. They were pushing toward it. And I noticed this with every set back we faced, every issue that came up in the lab.

I started paying attention to how teams moved. How they made decisions. How they handled setbacks. You can't diligence "grit" the same way you diligence IP, but you can feel it in a company's rhythm. You can feel it when someone's still thinking about the problem at all times – not because they have to, but because they want the science to work that badly.

2. The Innovation: Real differentiation

In the lab, I've run my share of molecular assays. I've done the benchwork. But I've also started asking a different kind of question: is this discovery we are working on building toward something new, or is it just a slightly faster way to do something old?

In my work now — both in wet lab settings and in the computational work I'm doing – I keep coming back to technical edge. Is there something here that fundamentally changes how we approach biology? Is this a new mechanism, a new system, a new way of designing?

There's a lot of biotech that sounds exciting but doesn't move the frontier. The best companies know the difference!

3. The Scale: will it matter?

The third question I started asking came from being part of Venture Lab at Penn, where I've worked alongside student founders trying to go from idea to launch. The thing no one tells you is that even great ideas can stall without a system to scale into. That's just as true in biotech.

I've realized that tools only matter if they're usable. Platforms only matter if they grow. You can design something brilliant, but if there's no real path to impact, to product, to patient, to platform — it doesn't hold.

Scalability isn't just about market size. It's about structure. Can this discovery expand? Can it compound?

So — what's my thesis?

I use it to learn. It might not be perfect, but it's what I've noticed. It's the pattern that's started to form after enough time in labs, in startups, and around people building things that matter.

Team. Innovation. Scale.

Are the founders relentless?

Is the science actually differentiated?

And will it matter?

I keep coming back to those three questions throughout all the work I do. Not because they give me all the answers, but because I feel that they help me see more clearly. I use them to think through companies, experiments, and even the tools I'm building. And every time I ask them, I learn something new — about the work, and about how I want to keep showing up in it.
