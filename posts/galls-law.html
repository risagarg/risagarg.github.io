My Thoughts on Gall's Law

October 19, 2025

Reading my systems theory textbook last month, I learned about Gall's Law, and it's completely changed how I think about ambitious projects. The law states: "A complex system that works is invariably found to have evolved from a simple system that worked. A complex system designed from scratch never works and cannot be patched up to make it work. You have to start over with a working simple system."

At first, reading this, I thought it was trivial. But the more I sat with it, the more it made sense to me. Gall wasn't just saying "start small and grow." He was making a claim that complex systems cannot be designed upfront and only come about through evolution. I can see this now as a constraint on what's possible in the realm of building, and not as advice.

What made this non-obvious to me is that it runs counter to how we're taught to approach problems. In school, I learned to plan comprehensively, anticipate edge cases, and design complete solutions. We admire grand visions and master plans all our lives. But Gall's Law suggests this approach is fundamentally doomed when dealing with complex systems – whether that's an ecosystem, a city, or an organization.

I started noticing this pattern everywhere. Amazon didn't start with a plan to sell everything; they sold books and expanded as they learned what worked. Facebook wasn't designed to be a global social network; it was a simple directory for Harvard students. The most successful software we use – Google, Stripe, Figma – all started by solving one narrow problem exceptionally well, then grew from there. Even looking at failed projects around me (including my own, too!), I realized the ones that struggled most were those that tried to build too much at once, designing for imagined future complexity rather than present reality.

The insight extends beyond business. I realized it explains why New Year's resolutions often fail, because we design complex systems of habits from scratch rather than evolving from one simple change that works. It's a principle about how change actually happens in the world.

To me, this law reveals a fundamental constraint on human agency. We like to believe that with enough intelligence and planning, we can design anything. Gall's Law suggests that for complex systems, this is false. Intelligence helps, but it cannot substitute for the evolutionary process of starting simple, learning what works, and building on that foundation.

This has practical implications for how I have been thinking about ideas I hear now. When I hear someone describe an elaborate plan, I look for what the simplest working version looks like, and whether there's a plausible path from that simple system to the grander vision. I've begun to be more interested in problems that seem narrow but have natural adjacencies for expansion than in problems that require solving everything at once. Most importantly, Gall's law has taught me that there's much that can only be discovered through iteration. Some knowledge can only come from building, failing, and adapting.
